## SECTION 1: EXTERNAL CLAIMS VERIFICATION

### 1.1 Mini Police Substations ("50+ Cities, 20-30% Crime Reduction")

#### Research Findings:

**VERDICT: PARTIALLY VERIFIED** - The model and outcomes are real, but precise claim requires nuance.

**What I Found:**

The campaign claims that mini police substations reduce crime by 20-30% and are proven in "50+ cities." My research shows:

‚úÖ **Community policing broadly (including mini substations) IS proven effective:**
- New Haven, CT: ~30% reduction in serious crime after foot patrol implementation (2012-2015)
- Research on hot spots policing and community policing shows significant crime reductions
- Police substations/mini-stations exist in major cities: Chicago (280+ neighborhood beat officers), NYC (77 precincts), Los Angeles, Seattle, Baltimore, Boston

‚ö†Ô∏è **However, the specific "20-30%" figure is more complex:**
- This figure appears to represent community policing effectiveness generally, not mini substations specifically
- A Montreal study found mixed evidence on police station crime deterrence
- A Newark BID study (Piza et al. 2020) found substations reduced burglary and motor vehicle theft, but didn't report 20-30% figures
- The effectiveness depends heavily on implementation context

üìä **Evidence Quality:** MODERATE-HIGH for community policing; LOW-MODERATE for specific mini substation attribution

**Recommendation:** Modify messaging to say "Community policing strategies including neighborhood substations have shown 20-30% crime reductions in multiple cities" rather than attributing it solely to mini substations.

### 1.5 Wellness Centers ROI ($1.80 vs $3.00 per $1 Spent)

#### Research Findings:

**VERDICT: BOTH FIGURES EXIST - NEED TO CHOOSE ONE ‚ö†Ô∏è**

**The $1.80 Figure:**
- Documents cite "Trust for America's Health research"
- I could NOT verify this specific $1.80 figure from TFAH
- However, a Chicago youth summer jobs study found: **$1.80 saved in criminal justice costs per $1 invested**
- This appears to be a different program type (youth employment, not wellness centers)

**What Trust for America's Health Actually Says:**
- TFAH's 2008 report "Prevention for a Healthier America" found:
- $10 per person investment in community prevention programs yields $16 billion savings in 5 years
- This equals **$5.60 return for every $1 invested**

**The $3.00 Figure:**
- Also commonly cited in public health literature
- Various programs show this ROI (tobacco cessation, immunizations, workplace wellness)
- Massachusetts tobacco cessation: **$3.12 medical savings per $1 spent**

**Other Community Health Center ROI Examples I Found:**
- Immunizations: $10.20 saved per $1 invested
- California tobacco control: $155 saved per $1 invested
- Various CHW diabetes programs: $1.09-$2.00 per $1 invested
- Cancer prevention (Cada Paso del Camino): $3.16 per $1 invested

**Evidence Quality:** HIGH for prevention programs generally; LOW for specific $1.80 wellness center claim

**CRITICAL INCONSISTENCY:**
- Campaign fact sheet lists BOTH figures: "$1.80 saved" (line 97) AND "$3 saved" (line 97 note)
- Policy documents consistently use $1.80
- The $1.80 figure may be conflating youth employment ROI with wellness center ROI

**Recommendation:**
1. Either use the **general prevention ROI range of $3-5 per $1** (well-supported)
2. OR find specific community health center ROI studies to cite
3. DO NOT continue citing $1.80 unless you can source it to wellness centers specifically
